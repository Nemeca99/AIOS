How is foundry coke made

Assistant
Foundry coke is a high-quality fuel used in the process of melting metals, such as iron and steel, in foundries. It is made by heating coal in an oxygen-deprived environment to drive off impurities and volatile compounds, leaving behind a porous, high-carbon material that burns hot and clean.

The process of making foundry coke typically involves the following steps:

1. Selection and preparation of coal: The type and quality of coal used to make foundry coke can have a significant impact on its properties and performance. Generally, coal with a high carbon content, low ash content, and low sulfur content is preferred. The coal is crushed and screened to remove any impurities and sized to meet the requirements of the coke ovens.

2. Charging the coke oven: The prepared coal is loaded into a series of tall, vertical coke ovens, which are sealed to create an oxygen-deprived environment. The ovens are typically heated to around 2000F (1100C) using a mixture of hot gases from combustion and waste heat from the cooling coke.

3. Coking: The coal is slowly heated in the coke oven over a period of several days, during which time it undergoes a complex series of physical and chemical changes. Volatile compounds are driven off, leaving behind a porous, high-carbon material called coke.

4. Quenching and handling: Once the coking process is complete, the hot coke is quenched with water to prevent it from continuing to burn. The coke is then removed from the ovens and cooled further before being crushed and screened to the desired size.

5. Testing and storage: The finished coke is tested for various properties, such as strength, porosity, and ash content, to ensure that it meets the required specifications. It is then stored in covered areas to prevent contamination and degradation until it is ready to be used as fuel in the foundry process.

Overall, the process of making foundry coke is complex and requires careful attention to detail to produce a high-quality fuel that meets the demanding requirements of the foundry industry.
